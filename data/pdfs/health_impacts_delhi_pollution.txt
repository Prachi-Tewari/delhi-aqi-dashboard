DOCUMENT: Health Impacts of Delhi's Air Pollution — Medical Research Summary
SOURCE: Compiled from peer-reviewed studies (Lancet, BMJ, Nature, NEJM) 2020–2025
TYPE: Health Impact Reference

=== RESPIRATORY HEALTH EFFECTS ===

PM2.5 AND LUNG DAMAGE:
PM2.5 particles (≤2.5 micrometers) are so small they penetrate deep into the alveoli — the tiny air sacs where oxygen enters the blood. Unlike larger particles that the body can filter through nose hair and mucus, PM2.5 bypasses all natural defenses.

A landmark study published in The Lancet Respiratory Medicine (2023) found that Delhi residents have 20–30% lower lung function (measured by FEV1) compared to people living in cities with clean air. The study tracked 5,000 individuals over 10 years.

Key findings on respiratory health:
- Asthma prevalence in Delhi: 12–15% (national average: 3–5%)
- COPD prevalence in Delhi non-smokers: 3× higher than expected from smoking alone
- Winter hospital admissions for respiratory distress increase 35–50% when AQI exceeds 300
- Children in Delhi show lung development equivalent to children 2–3 years younger in clean-air cities
- Long-haul exposure to PM2.5 > 60 µg/m³ causes irreversible fibrotic changes in lung tissue

OZONE AND RESPIRATORY IRRITATION:
Ground-level ozone (O3) is particularly insidious because it's invisible and its effects are delayed. Peak ozone occurs in the afternoon (1–4 PM) during sunny days when NO2 reacts with volatile organic compounds (VOCs).

Effects include:
- Airway inflammation within 1 hour of exposure above 100 µg/m³
- Reduced lung function that persists 24–48 hours after exposure
- Triggers asthma attacks even in people with mild, well-controlled asthma
- Long-term exposure linked to new-onset asthma in adults

=== CARDIOVASCULAR HEALTH EFFECTS ===

PM2.5 enters the bloodstream through the lungs and triggers systemic inflammation. Research from AIIMS Delhi published in the European Heart Journal (2024) demonstrated:

- Blood pressure increases 5–10 mmHg within hours of PM2.5 spike
- Heart attack (myocardial infarction) risk increases 5% per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 increase
- Stroke risk increases 1.5% per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 increase
- Atrial fibrillation episodes increase 7% during severe pollution episodes
- Blood becomes "stickier" (increased platelet aggregation), promoting clot formation
- Atherosclerosis progression accelerates — Delhi residents show 15% more arterial plaque than age-matched controls in Shimla

A 2025 meta-analysis in Nature Medicine found that cardiovascular mortality in Delhi increases by 2.4% for every 10 µg/m³ increase in daily average PM2.5 above 50 µg/m³.

=== NEUROLOGICAL EFFECTS ===

Emerging research shows PM2.5 and ultrafine particles can cross the blood-brain barrier:

- Cognitive decline: A study of 12,000 Delhi residents showed that chronic exposure to PM2.5 > 80 µg/m³ was associated with cognitive aging equivalent to 3–5 extra years
- Children's IQ: Delhi schoolchildren exposed to high PM2.5 scored 3–5 points lower on standardized IQ tests (Lancet Planetary Health, 2024)
- Dementia risk: Long-term PM2.5 exposure increases Alzheimer's risk by 40% (adjusted for other factors)
- Depression: Air pollution days correlate with 15% increase in depression-related emergency visits

=== PREGNANCY AND CHILD HEALTH ===

Pregnant women in Delhi face elevated risks:
- Low birth weight: 20% higher incidence when mother exposed to AQI > 200 during third trimester
- Preterm birth: 15% increased risk per 25 µg/m³ PM2.5 increase
- Gestational diabetes: Linked to NO2 exposure during second trimester
- PM2.5 crosses the placental barrier — found in fetal organs in autopsy studies

Children under 5:
- Respiratory infections: Leading cause of under-5 mortality in Delhi, with air pollution as a major contributing factor
- Delhi children have 43% higher rates of acute respiratory infections than the national average
- Permanent lung damage: Lungs exposed to high pollution during development (ages 0–18) never reach full capacity

=== CANCER RISK ===

The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC/WHO) classified outdoor air pollution as a Group 1 carcinogen in 2013. In Delhi:

- Lung cancer rates have increased 30% over the past decade, with a notable increase in non-smokers
- Non-smoker lung cancer now accounts for 40% of lung cancer cases at major Delhi hospitals (up from 20% two decades ago)
- Bladder cancer risk increases 15% with chronic exposure to PM2.5 > 50 µg/m³
- Benzene (from vehicle exhaust): Causes leukemia, found at 3–5× safe levels in Delhi traffic zones

=== ECONOMIC BURDEN ===

Air pollution's health costs in Delhi (Dalberg Advisors + CPCB, 2024):
- Annual healthcare costs: ₹2,500–3,000 crore ($300–360 million)
- Lost working days: 18–20 million person-days annually
- Productivity loss: ₹8,000–10,000 crore ($1–1.2 billion) annually
- Life years lost: 7–10 years average life expectancy reduction for Delhi residents

=== CIGARETTE EQUIVALENCE CALCULATIONS ===

The Berkeley Earth/Richard Muller methodology converts PM2.5 concentration to cigarette equivalence:
- Formula: Cigarettes/day = (PM2.5 in µg/m³) / 22
- At PM2.5 = 50 µg/m³ → ~2.3 cigarettes/day
- At PM2.5 = 100 µg/m³ → ~4.5 cigarettes/day
- At PM2.5 = 200 µg/m³ → ~9.1 cigarettes/day
- At PM2.5 = 300 µg/m³ → ~13.6 cigarettes/day
- At PM2.5 = 500 µg/m³ → ~22.7 cigarettes/day

This means a Delhi resident during winter (typical PM2.5 ~150) is effectively smoking 7 cigarettes daily just by breathing.

=== PROTECTIVE ACTIONS BY AQI LEVEL ===

AQI 0–50 (Good): No restrictions needed.
AQI 51–100 (Satisfactory): Sensitive individuals limit prolonged outdoor exercise.
AQI 101–200 (Moderate): 
  - Everyone: Reduce prolonged outdoor exertion
  - Sensitive groups: Limit outdoor activity, use N95 mask
  - Indoor: Close windows during peak hours (6-9 AM, 5-8 PM)

AQI 201–300 (Poor):
  - Everyone: Avoid prolonged outdoor activity, N95 mask outdoors
  - Sensitive groups: Stay indoors, run air purifier
  - Schools: Cancel outdoor sports/activities
  - Exercise: Move workouts indoors

AQI 301–400 (Very Poor):
  - Everyone: N95 mask mandatory outdoors, minimize outdoor time
  - Air purifiers: Essential for homes and offices
  - Schools: Consider closure for primary schools
  - Outdoor workers: Employers should provide masks and reduce shift hours

AQI 401–500 (Severe):
  - EMERGENCY: Stay indoors as much as possible
  - All schools shift to online classes
  - N95/N99 mask mandatory for any outdoor exposure
  - Multiple air purifiers running continuously
  - Seal windows/doors with damp towels if no purifier
  - Seek immediate medical help for breathing difficulty
  - Avoid cooking with gas if possible (adds indoor PM)
